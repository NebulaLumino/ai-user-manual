# Research Memo: Psychedelic Neuroscience: Psilocybin, MDMA & the Default Mode Network

## Overview

Psychedelic neuroscience is one of the most exciting and rapidly advancing frontiers in brain science. After decades of prohibition and stigma following the 1970s "War on Drugs," research on classic psychedelics (psilocybin, LSD, DMT) and entactogens (MDMA) has returned to mainstream academia, producing remarkable findings about the mechanisms of these compounds and their remarkable therapeutic potential for treatment-resistant depression, PTSD, addiction, and end-of-life anxiety.

### Classical Psychedelics: Mechanisms of Action

Classical psychedelics including psilocybin (the active compound in "magic mushrooms"), LSD, and DMT primarily exert their effects through agonism at serotonin 2A (5-HT2A) receptors. The 5-HT2A receptor is widely distributed throughout the cortex, with particularly high density in layer V pyramidal neurons of the prefrontal cortex—neurons that project widely to other cortical and subcortical regions and play a critical role in cortical information processing.

**The 5-HT2A receptor and cortical dynamics**: 5-HT2A receptor agonism increases glutamatergic signaling in the cortex by facilitating the release of glutamate from pyramidal neuron terminals, particularly through disinhibition of layer V pyramidal neurons. This enhanced glutamatergic signaling increases the entropy of cortical dynamics—disrupting the stereotyped, self-organized activity patterns that characterize normal waking consciousness.

**The entropy hypothesis**: Robin Carhart-Harris's "entropic brain" hypothesis proposes that psychedelic experience reflects a generalized increase in brain entropy—the number of possible functional configurations the brain can assume. Normal waking consciousness is characterized by relatively low entropy, with activity organized around stable attractor states (the default mode network). Psilocybin increases entropy, allowing the brain to explore a wider range of functional states—manifesting phenomenologically as altered perception, ego dissolution, and the experience of "anything possible."

### The Default Mode Network and Ego Dissolution

The Default Mode Network (DMN)—the set of brain regions active during rest and internally directed cognition—is perhaps the most replicated finding in social cognitive neuroscience. The DMN includes the medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC), posterior cingulate cortex (PCC), precuneus, angular gyrus, and hippocampus. It is associated with self-referential processing, mind-wandering, autobiographical memory, and the sense of being a unified, bounded self—the "ego."

A robust finding across multiple studies is that psilocybin and LSD significantly reduce DMN activity and connectivity. The PCC/precuneus—hub regions of the DMN—show particularly pronounced decreases in activity and connectivity. This reduction in DMN dominance is correlated with the subjective experience of "ego dissolution"—the loosening or loss of the sense of being a unified, bounded self.

**Why does DMN suppression feel meaningful?** The DMN is associated with the narrative self—the autobiographical story we tell about who we are. Reducing DMN activity may allow access to more primary, embodied forms of experience, including states that are interpreted as mystical, spiritual, or enlightenment experiences. The DMN may act as a "reality filter"—constraining the brain's natural tendency to impose narrative structure on experience—and psychedelic-induced DMN suppression may temporarily reduce this filtering.

**Integration and afterglow**: A key finding is that the therapeutic effects of psychedelics are not limited to the acute experience. A "psychedelic afterglow"—reduced DMN dominance and enhanced neuroplasticity—can persist for weeks after a single dose. This suggests that psychedelics may "reset" the brain's attractor landscape, opening a window of neuroplasticity during which therapeutic learning can occur.

### Psilocybin for Treatment-Resistant Depression

The most striking clinical application of psychedelic neuroscience is the use of psilocybin in treatment-resistant depression (TRD). Two randomized controlled trials by Robin Carhart-Harris at Imperial College London showed that two doses of psilocybin (with psychological support) produced rapid and sustained antidepressant effects in patients with TRD who had failed multiple antidepressant medications.

In the pivotal trial, patients received two sessions with psilocybin (25mg and 30mg, 1 week apart) with psychological support. The results showed: rapid antidepressant effects within 24-48 hours after dosing; sustained remission in many patients at 3-month follow-up; and no serious adverse events. These results are remarkable given that this population typically has decades of chronic depression and has failed multiple treatments.

**Mechanisms**: The antidepressant effect of psilocybin is associated with increased brain entropy (allow exploration of new mental states) and decreased DMN connectivity (reduced rumination and self-referential thinking—the cognitive hallmarks of depression). Functional connectivity studies show that psilocybin induces lasting changes in brain network organization—particularly increased global connectivity—that correlate with clinical improvement.

**Neuroplasticity**: Psilocybin enhances neuroplasticity by increasing BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) expression, promoting synaptogenesis, and enhancing neurogenesis. The 5-HT2A receptor activation leads to downstream signaling cascades (mTOR pathway activation) that promote the growth of new synaptic connections—a mechanism it shares with ketamine and other fast-acting antidepressants.

### MDMA: The Empathogen for PTSD

MDMA (3,4-methylenedioxymethamphetamine), known as "ecstasy" in its recreational form, is classified as an entactogen—a compound that produces feelings of emotional openness, empathy, and connection. Unlike classical psychedelics, MDMA primarily works through releasing serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine from presynaptic terminals (rather than direct receptor agonism), producing a characteristic empathogenic effect.

The combination of MDMA with psychotherapy—MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD—has shown remarkable efficacy in Phase 2 and Phase 3 clinical trials. In the Phase 3 trial (MAPS, 2021), 67% of participants no longer met PTSD criteria after two sessions with MDMA-assisted therapy, compared to 32% in the therapy-only group.

**Mechanism**: MDMA increases oxytocin (associated with trust and social bonding), reduces amygdala reactivity to threat-related stimuli, and enhances memory reconsolidation during therapy sessions. The drug produces a state of emotional safety that allows patients to revisit traumatic memories without becoming overwhelmed—facilitating the therapeutic processing of trauma that is otherwise prevented by fear.

### The Neural Signature of Mystical Experience

The subjective reports of psilocybin experiences closely resemble reports of naturally occurring mystical experiences—feelings of unity, sacredness, ineffability, and noetic quality ("insight beyond the intellect"). The question of whether psilocybin induces genuine mystical experiences, and whether these experiences mediate therapeutic benefit, is central to psychedelic neuroscience.

Neuroimaging studies show that mystical experience intensity correlates with: reduced DMN activity; increased global brain connectivity; changes in the thalamus (which gates information flow to cortex); and altered temporal lobe activity (particularly the hippocampus, associated with memory and the sense of temporal transcendence).

The subjective quality of mystical experience appears to be more important than the pharmacological specifics for therapeutic outcomes—patients who have the most profound mystical-type experiences show the greatest clinical benefit.

## AI Applications

### Predicting Response to Psychedelic Therapy

AI models trained on clinical, neuroimaging, psychological, genetic, and psychometric data can predict which patients will respond to psychedelic therapy, enabling better treatment matching. Baseline DMN connectivity, personality traits (openness), and psychological flexibility predict response.

### Analyzing Subjective Experience

Natural language processing applied to post-session narrative reports can quantify subjective experience dimensions (mystical, challenging, emotional) with high reliability, providing better measurement of the "psychedelic experience" than traditional rating scales.

### Brain Connectivity and Mechanism

Graph neural networks applied to functional connectivity data can identify the specific network configurations associated with therapeutic response—potentially revealing the "mechanism" of psychedelic therapy in a way that simple group comparisons cannot.

## Tools and Methods

### Neuroimaging
- fMRI: DMN and global connectivity changes
- EEG: Entropic measures, alpha suppression
- PET: Receptor occupancy and 5-HT2A binding
- MEG: Temporal dynamics of psychedelic experience

### Clinical Assessment
- Clinician-Administered PTSD Scale (CAPS) for PTSD trials
- GRID-Hamilton Depression Rating Scale (GRID-HAM) for depression trials
- Mystical Experience Questionnaire (MEQ30)
- Challenging Experience Questionnaire (CEQ)

## Challenges

### Safety

Psychedelic therapy requires careful screening for contraindications (personal or family history of psychosis, bipolar disorder) and controlled settings with trained therapists. The return of psychedelics to clinical use must be accompanied by robust safety protocols.

### Mechanism

Despite remarkable clinical results, the precise mechanism by which psilocybin and MDMA produce lasting therapeutic benefit remains unclear. Is it the acute mystical experience, the neuroplasticity window, the therapeutic context, or some combination? This matters for optimizing treatment protocols.

### Long-Term Effects

The long-term effects of psychedelic therapy—including potential risks of repeated use, effects on personality, and impacts on brain structure—are not fully characterized. Most trials involve only 1-3 doses.

## Ethics

### Access and Equity

If psychedelic therapies are approved, their cost and limited availability may make them accessible only to wealthy patients—creating inequities in who can benefit from these remarkable treatments.

### Indigenous Knowledge

Many psychedelics have been used in indigenous ceremonial contexts for millennia. The appropriation of these compounds by Western medicine raises questions about benefit-sharing and cultural respect.

### Post-Treatment Support

Psychedelic therapy requires extensive preparation before sessions and integration support afterward. Without adequate support, the therapeutic window opened by psychedelics may not translate into lasting change.

## Future Directions

### Broader Clinical Applications

Psilocybin and MDMA are being tested for conditions beyond depression and PTSD—including anorexia, OCD, alcohol use disorder, and existential distress in terminal illness. The scope of psychedelic medicine is expanding rapidly.

### Combination Approaches

Combining psychedelic therapy with other interventions—ketamine, mindfulness training, cognitive behavioral therapy—may enhance and extend therapeutic benefit.

### Next-Generation Compounds

Novel 5-HT2A agonists and analogs are being developed with potentially more favorable pharmacokinetics, shorter durations, or reduced hallucinogenic effects while maintaining therapeutic benefit.
